Overflowing Police Precinct Parking Lots Spark Troubles

Community advocates bring attention to another piece in Manhattan's mammoth parking puzzle.

By Matthew Danbury

The horns of two fire trucks blared one chilly Manhattan morning as they approached a clot of traffic at 100th and Amsterdam on their way to an emergency. For minutes, they remained stalled as cars and vans shuffled awkwardly to attempt to clear a path.

At the front of the blockage stood a double-parked delivery vehicle that had reduced traffic on the narrow street down to a single lane.

This stretch of 100th street is home to both the 24th Police Precint and Engine Company 76, Ladder Company 22, and Battalion 11 of the FDNY.

While the block already has several dozen spots dedicated for the precinct, officers often park and double park their vehicles on the rest of the street as well.

On this day in particular, several precinct affiliated vehicles were blocking the delivery driveway where the truck in question was supposed to unload.

This incident is emblematic of a pattern of complaints from residents of the Upper West Side of precinct parking spilling over into surrounding streets, leading to frustrating and potentially dangerous situations.

These precinct overflows are, of course, just one part of a greater problem: parking in New York City is scarce and has only gotten scarcer in recent years.

The coronavirus pandemic ushered in the widespread adoption of dining sheds, a shift to using e-commerce for everything from groceries to durable goods, and a fear of public transportation that drove historic increases in vehicle registration.

With more vehicles on the street and fewer places to put them, non-emergency complaints about vehicles blocking driveways, bike lanes, traffic lanes, parked vehicles, fire hydrants, and sidewalks have skyrocketed.

This makes it all the more tricky to find additional curb space to dedicate for precint parking. At a Community Board 7 Transportation Comittee Meeting in March Donald Press, a resident of the Upper West Side who leads the 81st Street Block Association, presented a study of the issue he conducted which showed that there is a need for about 20-25 extra slots for vehicles affiliated with the 20th police precinct alone.

But the prospect of further shrinking the community's stock of street parking spaces elicited groans from other attendees who expressed discontents about feeling priced out of car ownership due to exorbitant garage rates.

Where everyone agreed was that residents are exasperated by the gridlock, delays, and dangers these infractions cause."The deeper problem is that it's basically a safety safety safety problem," said Press, citing examples of blocked fire hydrants, traffic, and turn lanes that he observed.
