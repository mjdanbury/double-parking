As NYC Parking Options Dwindle, Illegal Parking Complaints Have Soared

By Matthew Danbury

Parking in New York City is scarce and has only gotten scarcer in recent years. The coronavirus pandemic ushered in the widespread adoption of dining sheds, a shift to using e-commerce for everything from groceries to durable goods, and a fear of public transportation that drove historic increases in vehicle registration.

The horns of two fire trucks blare one chilly Manhattan morning as they approach a clot of traffic.

With two cars parked in front of a designated delivery driveway, a truck has double parked to make its delivery, reducing both directions of traffic to a single lane.

As the sirens continue to wail, drivers shuffle awkwardly around trying to make way.

Meanwhile, more and more cars pile up behind them.

It is several minutes before this fiasco sorts itself out and the trucks can cotninue on thier way to the emergency they have been called to respond to.

With more vehicles on the street and fewer places to put them, many drivers are routinely making the decision to park illegally—blocking driveways, bike lanes, traffic lanes, parked vehicles, fire hydrants, and sidewalks with their trucks, cars, vans, and SUVs.

Many New Yorkers are exasperated by the gridlock and delays these infractions cause, and pedestrians and motorists alike are terrified by the deadly blindspots at crossings and intersections that these illegally parked vehicles create.

In the outer boroughs, property owners are paving over their lawns to add parking spaces—increasing flood water runoff during heavy storms, and obstructing firefighters and EMTs from accessing, and illegally cutting sections out of the curb for a new driveway — taking one more parking spot off the street and further exacerbating the issue.
