Overflowing Police Precinct Parking Lots Spark Troubles

Community advocates bring attention to another piece in Manhattan's mammoth parking puzzle.

By Matthew Danbury

The horns of two fire trucks blared one chilly Manhattan morning as they approached a clot of traffic at 100th and Amsterdam on their way to an emergency. For minutes, they remained stalled as cars and vans shuffled awkwardly to attempt to clear a path.

At the front of the blockage stood a double-parked delivery vehicle that had reduced traffic on the narrow street down to a single lane.

This stretch of 100th street is home to both the 24th Police Precint and Engine Company 76, Ladder Company 22, and Battalion 11 of the FDNY.

While the block already has several dozen spots dedicated for the precinct, officers often park and double park their vehicles on the rest of the street as well.

On this day in particular, several precinct affiliated vehicles were blocking the delivery driveway where the truck in question was supposed to unload.

This incident is emblematic of a pattern of complaints from residents of the Upper West Side of precinct parking spilling over into surrounding streets, leading to frustrating and potentially dangerous situations.

This much was clear from a presentation that Manhattan resident Donald Press gave at the Community Board 7 Transportation Comittee Meeting in March.

These precinct overflows are, of course, just one part of a greater problem: parking in New York City is scarce and has only gotten scarcer in recent years. The coronavirus pandemic ushered in the widespread adoption of dining sheds, a shift to using e-commerce for everything from groceries to durable goods, and a fear of public transportation that drove historic increases in vehicle registration.

Complaints about illegal parking are up across the board as drivers are routinely making the decision to block driveways, bike lanes, traffic lanes, parked vehicles, fire hydrants, and sidewalks with their trucks, cars, vans, and SUVs.

Many New Yorkers are exasperated by the gridlock and delays these infractions cause, and pedestrians and motorists alike are terrified by the deadly blindspots at crossings and intersections that these illegally parked vehicles create.

In the outer boroughs, property owners are paving over their lawns to add parking spaces—increasing flood water runoff during heavy storms, and obstructing firefighters and EMTs from accessing, and illegally cutting sections out of the curb for a new driveway — taking one more parking spot off the street and further exacerbating the issue.
